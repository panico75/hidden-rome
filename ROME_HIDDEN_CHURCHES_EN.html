Rome: Hidden Churches and Treasures

A one-day walking itinerary among the lesser-known wonders of Rome

Introduction

This itinerary will guide you through some of Rome's most fascinating and lesser-known churches, along with archaeological sites of great importance. Starting from Piazza Fanti near Termini Station, you will explore the ancient heart of the city, discovering hidden treasures, underground crypts, and magnificent Byzantine mosaics.

The itinerary is designed to be mainly walked, with some public transport options if necessary. The overall duration is a full day, with breaks for breakfast, lunch, and dinner in authentic Roman establishments away from the most crowded tourist circuits.

Day Itinerary

Basilica of Saints Sylvester and Martin in the Hills

9:00 - 9:30

This ancient basilica, dating back to the 4th century, hides underground treasures of great historical value. From the central nave, you can descend on foot into the crypt through a clearly visible staircase. The crypt, renovated in the 17th century, preserves the relics of martyrs from the Catacombs of Priscilla. From here, through another staircase, you can access the Titulus Equitii, a 3rd-century Roman villa adapted for Christian worship in the early 4th century.

Address

Viale del Monte Oppio 28

Hours

8:30 AM - 12:45 PM, 2:00 PM - 5:00 PM

Entrance

Free FREE

Visit time

30 minutes

Why it's unique: It is one of the oldest churches in Rome and preserves archaeological remains of great value, including a Roman domus and thermal structures from the 3rd century. This archaeological stratification offers a unique opportunity to travel through time, from the original Roman structures to the current basilica with its 24 ancient Corinthian columns and coffered ceiling from 1560.

Basilica of Saint Peter in Chains

10:00 - 10:30

Famous for housing Michelangelo's Moses, part of Pope Julius II's funerary monument. This sculpture is considered one of Michelangelo's absolute masterpieces, carved from a single block of marble. The statue depicts Moses seated, with a flowing beard and intense gaze, while holding the tablets of the law under his arm. An interesting detail is that Michelangelo, a few years after creating a first version, decided to modify the position of the face and legs to give the sculpture a greater sense of dynamism and vitality.

Address

Piazza di San Pietro in Vincoli 4A

Hours

8:00 AM - 12:30 PM, 3:00 PM - 7:00 PM

Entrance

Free FREE

Visit time

30 minutes

Why it's unique: Besides the famous Moses, the church also houses the chains that, according to tradition, bound Saint Peter during his imprisonment in Jerusalem. The chains are preserved in a reliquary under the main altar and are displayed to the public on August 1st each year.

Basilica of Saint Clement

11:00 - 12:00

This basilica offers a fascinating journey through three levels of history: the current 12th-century church, a 4th-century basilica beneath it, and at the lowest level, a 1st-century Roman building and a 2nd-century Mithraeum. The descent through these layers allows visitors to literally walk through different historical periods, from ancient Rome to the Middle Ages.

The 12th-century apse mosaic is one of the most beautiful in Rome, and the underground Mithraeum is one of the best preserved in the world. In the 4th-century basilica, there are also frescoes with some of the earliest examples of written Italian language.

Afternoon Itinerary

Basilica of Saint Praxedes

2:00 - 2:30

This 9th-century church contains some of the most beautiful Byzantine mosaics in Rome, second only to those in Ravenna. The Chapel of Saint Zeno, commissioned by Pope Paschal I, is entirely covered with golden mosaics and is known as the "Garden of Paradise" for its splendor.

Address

Via di Santa Prassede 9

Hours

7:30 AM - 12:00 PM, 4:00 PM - 6:30 PM

Entrance

Free FREE

Visit time

30 minutes

Why it's unique: The church houses a fragment of the column to which Jesus was allegedly tied during his flagellation, brought from Jerusalem in 1223. The Byzantine mosaics in the apse and the Chapel of Saint Zeno are extraordinary examples of 9th-century art and are among the best preserved in Rome.

Basilica of Saint Mary Major

3:00 - 3:45

One of Rome's four major basilicas, it contains magnificent 5th-century mosaics that are among the oldest representations of the Virgin Mary after the Council of Ephesus (431 AD), which proclaimed her "Mother of God." The basilica also houses a relic believed to be from Jesus' manger.

Address

Piazza di Santa Maria Maggiore 42

Hours

7:00 AM - 7:00 PM

Entrance

Free FREE

Visit time

45 minutes

Why it's unique: It is the only major basilica that has preserved its early Christian structure, despite later renovations. The coffered ceiling is gilded with the first gold brought from the Americas, donated by the Spanish monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella. The Borghese Chapel contains the icon of "Salus Populi Romani," one of the most venerated Marian images in Rome.

Basilica of Saint Pudentiana

4:15 - 4:45

Considered the oldest place of Christian worship in Rome, this church stands on the site of the house of Senator Pudens, who according to tradition hosted Saint Peter. The apse mosaic, dating from the late 4th century, is the oldest Christian mosaic in Rome and depicts Christ teaching the apostles.

Address

Via Urbana 160

Hours

Why it's unique: The apse mosaic is of exceptional historical and artistic importance, showing a transition between Roman and Byzantine styles. The church also contains parts of the original Roman house, visible in the lower level.

Basilica of Saint Mary of the Angels and Martyrs

5:15 - 5:45

Designed by Michelangelo within the ruins of the Baths of Diocletian, this unique church incorporates the tepidarium (warm room) of the ancient Roman baths. The church also contains the meridian line created by Francesco Bianchini in 1702, a solar observatory used to measure the time and determine the exact date of Easter.

Address

Piazza della Repubblica

Hours

7:00 AM - 6:30 PM

Why it's unique: It is one of the few examples of a church built within ancient Roman ruins, preserving the original structure. The meridian line on the floor is still functional and is one of the largest astronomical instruments in the world.

Travel Tips

  • Appropriate clothing: To visit churches, make sure your shoulders and knees are covered. Bring a scarf or light shawl that can be useful if needed.
  • Church hours: Always check opening hours before visiting, especially for churches that might be closed during religious celebrations.
  • Water: Bring a reusable water bottle. Rome is full of "nasoni," public drinking fountains where you can refill your bottle.
  • Comfortable shoes: The itinerary involves about 5-6 km of walking in total, so wear comfortable shoes.
  • Tickets: For the Baths of Caracalla, the Basilica of San Clemente (underground), and the Domus Aurea, it is advisable to purchase tickets in advance to avoid queues.
  • Weather: In case of bad weather, consider using public transport between some of the more distant stops.
  • Safety: As in any major tourist city, be aware of pickpockets, especially in crowded areas.
  • Respect: Remember that you are visiting places of worship, so maintain respectful and quiet behavior.
  • Photography: In many churches, it is forbidden to take photos with flash. Always check if photography is allowed before doing so.
  • Reservations: For the more popular restaurants, consider booking in advance, especially for dinner.
